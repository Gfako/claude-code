# How to Write a YouTube Video Script (+ Template & Example)

Most of the YouTube script feedback I give comes down to the same handful of problems. The intro runs too long. The hook describes the video instead of selling it. The middle wanders. The CTA tries to do three things at once. And nobody can tell whether they're stuck on the script or on the delivery, so the next video gets re-shot when it should have been re-written.

I've been writing scripts for the Synthesia channel for a few years now. The longer I do it, the more convinced I am that the script does more work than anything else in the production. Cameras matter. Lighting matters. Whatever editing software you use matters a bit. The first 200 words of the script matter more than all of them combined.

This post is the process I actually use, broken into nine steps. Not the polished, six-bullet version you'll see on most blogs. Some of the steps sound obvious. A couple of them will probably sound wrong on first read. They work.

A note on who this is for: most of my examples involve marketing teams, in-house producers, content strategists, and founders running a brand channel. If you're a solo creator, take what's useful and skip the corporate bits.

## How to write a YouTube video script in 9 steps

The sequence matters more than people realise. Each step is meant to set up the next one. The single most common reason scripts come out flat: people skip ahead to writing dialogue before they've worked out what the video is for.

### Step 1: Start with an idea you'd actually click on

Before I write a single word, I make myself finish this sentence:

***"After watching this video, the viewer will be able to, understand, or feel X."***

One thing only. If I can't fill that in clearly, the video doesn't have a point yet and no amount of clever writing later will save it.

After the idea, I rough out a working title. Not the final, polished version (titles change in editing all the time), but something close enough to give the script a target. I learned the hard way that if you start writing without a title, you end up writing about three different videos that share a topic.

The part most teams struggle with here is choosing what to leave out. Brand teams in particular want a single video to explain the product, generate demand, address an objection, *and* welcome new viewers. Pick one. The others will get their own videos. A script that tries to serve four masters serves none of them.

To make this concrete: here's an example of an idea that did the work for me before I wrote anything else, *[How to translate any video into any language with lip sync](https://www.synthesia.io/features/ai-dubbing).* Translation and localization were already at the top of basically every customer's wishlist, so the idea had built-in pull before I touched the script. I supported it with a stat that framed the size of the problem (70% of business content online is in English, and only about 19% of people speak it). That's a single sentence and it does an enormous amount of work.

The takeaway buried in that example is something I wish someone had told me three years ago: a sharp idea does some of the persuasion before the thumbnail, title, or hook get a turn. Hooks are amplifiers. Thumbnails are amplifiers. The idea is the signal they're amplifying. If the signal is weak, no amount of amplification will help.

**Takeaway:** Write a one-sentence goal for the video before anything else. If you can't say it cleanly in one breath, don't move to the script yet.

### Step 2: Write your hook before anything else

The first 15 seconds decide whether the rest of the script matters. YouTube treats the first 30 seconds as its own retention metric in Studio analytics ([they call it Intro retention](https://support.google.com/youtube/answer/9314415)), and the official [YouTube Creator Playbook](https://blog.youtube/creator-and-artist-stories/youtube-creator-playbook-tips-first-15/) explicitly names the first 15 seconds as the hook window.

A good example to study, if you'll forgive the self-reference, is *the [Synthesia "How to make AI videos in 5 minutes" video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VY0HP6H9AY0).*

In those first 15 seconds the viewer gets three things at once. A clear outcome (turn any idea into an engaging video). An objection removed (you don't need video editing skills). A credibility signal (used by most of the Fortune 100). The intro itself was generated with an [AI avatar](https://www.synthesia.io/features/avatars) using [Synthesia's script-to-video workflow](https://www.synthesia.io/tools/script-to-video-maker), which means the hook *is* the product demo. The viewer doesn't have to imagine what the output looks like; they're watching it.

That kind of compression is what a good hook does. It states the outcome, kills the main objection, proves you're worth listening to, and shows the thing working, all before asking the viewer to commit to a few more minutes.

I write the hook before anything else. Before the outline. Before I've decided what the body will cover. Two formats keep working for me:

The **promise hook** tells the viewer exactly what they're about to get, and why now. Something like, *"In the next five minutes, I'll show you the script structure that doubled our channel's watch time."*

The **in-media-res hook** is a screenwriting term that just means starting in the middle of the action. You drop the viewer into the most interesting moment of the video, then loop back to set it up. *"This is the slide that nearly cost us the deal. Here's what we changed."* It's an older technique (Homer used it), and it still works because curiosity is older than YouTube.

Whichever format you use, the hook has to leave something unresolved. A teased reveal, a question without an answer, a claim that needs proof. If your hook fully describes the video, it's a headline. Headlines earn the click and then disappear. A hook holds the viewer.

The promise hook is easier to land on the first try, so start there. The in-media-res version is high-leverage when it works, but the reveal has to actually pay off; if it doesn't, you'll lose viewers faster than a flat hook would have.

**Takeaway:** Write the hook first. The strongest hooks state an outcome, remove an objection, prove you're worth listening to, and leave something unresolved.

### Step 3: Set up the gap between expectation and what you'll deliver

Once the hook is written, the next job is to plant a small piece of psychology that keeps the viewer watching: the gap between what they expect and what you're going to deliver.

People keep watching when reality beats their expectations, even a little. They drop off when reality matches their assumption. So the script needs to plant an expectation and beat it.

In practice this means, in the 30 seconds after your hook, naming the common-sense answer the viewer would give if you asked them, then promising a different one. *"Most teams try to fix retention by editing harder. That doesn't work. The fix is upstream, in the script."* The viewer now knows you're going against the obvious answer, and they'll stick around at least until you prove it.

This is also how you avoid what I think of as the dead-on-arrival first body point. If your first real argument is something the viewer has heard before, retention drops quickly. The first point of the body has to be either new, sharper than other people's version, or framed in a way the viewer hasn't encountered.

**Takeaway:** Inside the first minute, name the common-sense answer to your question, then promise a different one. The gap is what keeps the viewer watching.

### Step 4: Outline in bullet points, then order them so the second-best point goes first

Once the hook and the setup are in place, list every section of the video as a single bullet. Just the bullets; no full sentences yet. Using the Synthesia tutorial I mentioned earlier as a worked example, the outline for that [tutorial-style video](https://www.synthesia.io/tools/tutorial-video-maker) looked roughly like this:

- Hook (turn any idea into an engaging video in minutes, no editing skills)
- Start with the Assistant (open the dashboard, drop in a prompt or file, pick a template)
- Customise your AI avatar (costume, location, actions)
- Edit the script and turn the video interactive
- Add screen recordings and let the team collaborate
- Translate the video into 140+ languages, with lip sync
- CTA and outro (next tutorial: create your own AI avatar clone)

Now the bit most guides miss. The order of your body points matters far more than people give it credit for.

The rule that surprises people: **lead with your second-best point, not your strongest.** Then your best. Then your third best. Then fourth, and so on.

The instinct most creators have is to lead with the strongest argument, because they're worried viewers will leave before they hear it. What actually happens is the opposite. Open with the best material and the next point feels weaker by comparison; viewers feel the drop and drift away. Open with a strong-but-not-strongest point, then deliver something noticeably better, and you create a pattern of "this keeps getting better" that keeps people watching to see what's next. Albums work the same way. The single is rarely track one.

If the outline already feels boring on paper, the finished video will be boring on camera. Move things around until you'd watch it yourself. Moving a bullet is essentially free; moving a paragraph takes ten minutes; reshooting a section takes a day. Edit ruthlessly while it's cheap.

**Takeaway:** Lead the body with your second-best point, so the viewer feels the video getting stronger. Save the best for two-thirds in.

### Step 5: Build each body point as a value loop

For each point in the body, give the viewer three things, in this order:

1. **What it is.** Explain the point as simply as you can manage. A single sentence is usually enough.
2. **How to do it.** Show the application: a specific example, a tactic, a real situation, anything concrete.
3. **Why it matters.** Connect it back to the outcome the viewer came for. What changes if they apply this?

Most YouTube videos do step one and stop, which is why they feel thin. The "how" and the "why" are what move a point from interesting to useful, and useful is what gets people to keep watching (and also, incidentally, what gets the video shared).

One more pattern I'd add here, which is harder to describe but worth chasing: every body section should zoom out and zoom in. Big picture, then tactical, then back to big picture. The rhythm of going wide, then narrow, then wide again is what makes a 10-minute video feel like four.

**Takeaway:** Every body point should explain what it is, show how to apply it, and tell the viewer why it matters.

### Step 6: Write the dialogue the way you'd actually speak it

Writing for video is much closer to writing a podcast than writing an essay. The mistakes that creep in when people forget this are usually the same three.

First, contractions. Always. "You'll" rather than "you will." "Don't" rather than "do not." Nobody says "do not" out loud in conversation, so it sounds robotic the moment it's read aloud.

Second, sentence length. Keep every sentence short enough to say in one breath. If you stumble on a sentence when reading it out loud, split it. This is one of the few rules I've never seen exceptions to.

Third, read everything out loud as you write. Not at the end as a final check; *while you're writing.* The second I stop reading my own sentences aloud, they start getting longer and stiffer. I don't know why; it just happens.

I keep a personal blacklist of phrases that show up when my brain is on autopilot at the keyboard: "furthermore," "moreover," "in conclusion," "in today's fast-paced world." If I catch any of them in a draft, I take that as a sign I've stopped paying attention and I rewrite the paragraph.

A trick I stole from podcasters: read the script aloud to an empty room. If you'd be embarrassed if a friend walked in halfway through and heard you, the line is too formal. Rewrite it until it sounds like something you'd actually say.

**Takeaway:** If you can't say it out loud without stumbling, the viewer can't follow it. Speak the script as you write.

### Step 7: Mark pattern interrupts every 30 to 45 seconds

A pattern interrupt is anything that breaks the visual or audio rhythm of the video. A cut to a new angle, a graphic, a sound effect, a tone shift, a quick aside to camera. Each one resets the viewer's attention, which is why high-retention channels do them constantly.

I mark them inline in the script, in italics. Something like *cut to b-roll of customer office, graphic showing the retention curve overlay, aside to camera saying "and yes, I tried this myself."* Nothing fancy.

For a 10-minute video that's roughly 13 to 20 interrupts. It sounds like a lot until you actually watch a high-retention channel back with the markers in mind; MrBeast averages something close to one cut every two seconds. You don't have to go that hard. But "every 30 seconds" is the floor, not the goal.

If you're working with an editor, they'll cut exactly what's in the script, no more. If you're your own editor, which most B2B creators are these days, past-you is going to need to leave future-you something to work with. Tools like [Synthesia's AI video editor](https://www.synthesia.io/tools/ai-video-editor) make it easy to keep or drop a marked interrupt at the timeline level, so the cost of an extra marker in the script is essentially zero. The cost of a flat 30 seconds in the finished video is a chunk of your retention curve.

**Takeaway:** Mark interrupts every 30 to 45 seconds at minimum, whether you have an editor or you are one. Over-mark.

### Step 8: End with a CTA that feels native, not pushy

One ask. Not three. "Subscribe," *or* "watch the next video," *or* "drop a comment with your worst script story." Pick one and make it specific. Three CTAs in 30 seconds is the same as zero conversions, and I've checked.

What makes a CTA actually land is whether it feels like a natural next step from something the video has been talking about. If the body has been about how hard it is to write good scripts at scale, a soft mention of a template or tool that solves that exact problem reads as helpful. If the CTA swerves into something the video hasn't set up, viewers feel the gear shift and the conversion rate craters.

Keep the outro short. A sign-off, a thank-you, gone. Long outros are where viewers leave before [the algorithm registers the watch as complete](https://www.searchenginewatch.com/2012/10/22/youtube-algorithm-change-time-watched-key-to-higher-video-search-rankings/), which is a quiet way to bleed watch time across a whole channel.

**Takeaway:** One ask, tied to a problem the body already raised, delivered in under 30 seconds. Outro under 10.

### Step 9: Read it aloud, time it, trim it

Read the whole script out loud at a natural pace. Wherever you stumble, simplify. Wherever you get bored, cut. Then time the read.

At a natural pace, one minute of finished video runs to [roughly 160 to 180 words](https://www.isca-archive.org/interspeech_2006/yuan06_interspeech.html). If you wrote a 1,500-word script for a five-minute video, you're going to talk too fast or run long. Either way, trim before you record. Trimming in post is twice the work, and post-shoot you're emotionally attached to footage.

My honest experience: first drafts are always too long. I now write deliberately to 110% of target length so I can cut to the bone in the read-aloud pass. The video gets better every time I cut.

**Takeaway:** Read aloud. Time the read. Cut anything you stumble on. The version that ships should be tighter than the version you finished writing.

## A YouTube script template you can copy

Fill in the brackets, then rewrite for your voice.

*Video title.*

*Target length: X minutes.*

*Goal: after watching, the viewer will X.*

*Hook (0:00 to 0:15).* One sentence that tells the viewer what they'll get, or drops them into the most interesting moment of the video. Then one sentence that gives them a reason to stay, usually a teased reveal or an unanswered question.

*Setup (0:15 to 0:45).* Briefly name what most people get wrong about the topic, then promise a different angle. This is the expectation gap. Two or three sentences.

*Body.* Section by section, each one in the same shape: name the point, show how to apply it, say why it matters. Mark a pattern interrupt every 30 to 45 seconds in italics. Order the points so the second-best comes first, then the best, then the third.

*CTA (last 30 seconds).* One specific ask, ideally tied to a problem the body already raised.

*Outro (last 10 seconds).* A short sign-off, then out.

## How long should a YouTube video script be?

There isn't a universal answer; it depends on the format. Explainers and educational videos tend to land best between 5 and 10 minutes. Brand and product videos work in the 90-second to 4-minute range. Shorts under 60 seconds. The principle that holds across all of them is density: every second has to earn its place.

Once you know your target length, work backwards to a word count. A natural speaking pace is [roughly 160 to 180 words a minute](https://www.isca-archive.org/interspeech_2006/yuan06_interspeech.html), which accounts for pauses and B-roll. So a 2-minute video runs 320 to 360 words, a 5-minute video sits at 800 to 900, a 10-minute video is 1,600 to 1,800, and a 15-minute video is 2,400 to 2,700.

Treat those as upper bounds. My personal rule is to write to roughly 110% of target on purpose, then cut about 10% in the read-aloud pass. Almost every video I've made gets stronger when I cut, never weaker.

## A full YouTube script example

Here's a complete annotated 90-second script that runs end to end (hook, setup, body, CTA, outro). It exists to show the value loop, the ordering rule, and a native CTA all working at the same time.

*Title: "The script change that doubled our YouTube watch time."*

*Goal: marketing teams understand the one structural fix that lifts retention.*

*Hook (0:00 to 0:10).* "Last year we re-recorded the same product video three times. Same script, same presenter, same shots. Watch time barely moved. Then we changed one structural thing in the script and our average watch time doubled."

*Cut to a side-by-side retention curve, before and after.*

*Setup (0:10 to 0:25).* "Most teams try to lift retention by editing harder. Faster cuts. More graphics. Tighter sound design. None of that worked for us. The fix wasn't in the edit at all; it was in the script, specifically in the order of the body."

*Body (0:25 to 1:10).*

*Section 1, second-best point.* "We used to open the body with our strongest argument, because we were worried viewers would leave before they heard it. Turns out that's the worst thing you can do. Lead with your second-best point and you create a feeling of momentum. The viewer feels the video getting stronger."

*Cut to a graphic of three bars in ascending order.*

*Section 2, the best point.* "Then put the best argument in the middle. Not at the start, not at the end. Right where the viewer is committed but still curious. That's where a great point pays the biggest dividend in watch time."

*Aside to camera: "We tested this on six videos in a row. Same effect every time."*

*Section 3, the third-best point.* "Here's the part no one tells you: the third best point goes last in the body, not the second strongest. The progression we tested that worked best was second, first, fourth, third."

*CTA (1:10 to 1:25).* "If you want a template with this body order baked in, we keep a working version in our [free script template library](https://www.synthesia.io/post/free-video-script-templates). It's the same one we use internally."

*Outro (1:25 to 1:30).* "That's the fix. See you next week."

What to notice in this script:

- The hook makes a specific, falsifiable claim ("watch time doubled") and pays it off immediately with a visual.
- The setup names the common-sense answer ("edit harder") and rejects it, which opens an expectation gap.
- Every body point follows the value loop pattern: what the rule is, how to apply it, why it works.
- The CTA references a problem the body already raised (the body order is hard to figure out), so the link lands as a continuation of the video rather than a sales pitch.
- The outro is five seconds.

## How do you write a script for YouTube Shorts?

A Shorts script isn't a shorter long-form script; it's a different format. Shortened long-form scripts almost always get buried by the algorithm.

Three structural rules to keep in mind:

The first is hook in the first one to three seconds. Not 15. Vertical viewers swipe instantly, so the first sentence has to land before the camera has finished settling.

The second is no introduction. None. No "hey everyone," no name card, no explanation of what the Short is about. The Short *is* the value, so start with it.

The third is loop the ending. End on a sentence that makes the first sentence make more sense, so the algorithm replays it. Replays count as watch time, and watch time is what the Shorts algorithm rewards.

A Shorts template, written plainly: open with one sentence that creates an information gap, deliver the payoff in two or three short beats over the next 20 seconds (with on-screen text reinforcing the key phrases), and close with a line that loops back to the opener. Target script length is 50 to 75 words; anything longer and you'll talk too fast on camera.

## YouTube script formats and when to use each one

There are a handful of script formats in common use. Most YouTube videos don't need anything fancy, but knowing the options helps you pick the right one for what you're making.

- **Bulleted outline plus dialogue.** A simple list of sections with the spoken lines underneath. The format I use for the vast majority of videos. Fast to write, easy to share with a stakeholder, easy to edit. Best for explainers, talking-head videos, founder messages, and product demos.
- **AV (Audio/Visual).** Two columns: audio on one side, what's on screen on the other. Best for interviews, sit-down product reviews, and any video where the audio track and the visual track are doing different things.
- **VAG (Visual/Audio/Graphics).** AV with a third column for graphics. Best for training videos and educational explainers that lean heavily on charts, on-screen text, or overlays.
- **Sequential Flow.** A single running document with action lines, dialogue, and direction interleaved. Best for high-production work, short films, narrative-driven videos, anything with proper scene work.
- **Voiceover script.** Dialogue written as narration, with timestamps and visual cues noted in italics. Best for B-roll-heavy videos where the speaker is never on camera.
- **Interview script.** A question list with optional cue lines. Best for podcasts, founder interviews, and customer story videos. The script is a guide, not a wall.
- **Shorts script.** Hook, payoff, loop, as described above. Best for repurposing long-form into clipped pieces or for native vertical content.

If you're shooting alone or with a small team and you're not sure where to start, default to the bulleted outline plus dialogue. It's the fastest to write, the easiest to refine, and it transfers cleanly to any of the other formats once you grow into them.

## Can you use AI to write YouTube scripts without sounding generic?

Yes, but only if you treat AI as a draft engine and not as a writer. When teams let AI generate end to end, the output reads like AI. When they use AI for the parts that benefit from speed and bring their own voice to the rest, it works.

The parts of the workflow where I actually use AI:

For outlining, I'll often give the tool a goal and a topic and ask for five to ten angle options. Maybe one of them is interesting. I take that and rewrite. The other nine are useful as proof that the obvious angles are obvious.

For hook variations, I'll ask for ten alternate hooks for a topic. One is usually salvageable. I rewrite it in my voice.

For first drafts of sections I'm planning to heavily edit anyway, AI is fastest at producing "okay" first drafts, and I'd rather rewrite an okay draft than stare at a blank page.

For cutting, I'll paste my draft and ask which three sentences add the least value. Surprisingly useful. I'm too attached to my own writing to see this clearly.

The parts where AI consistently hurts:

Voice. Anything written end to end by AI sounds generic, and viewers can tell within a sentence. Current models share the same tells, and audiences are getting better at spotting them by the month.

Specifics. AI invents stats, quotes, and customer examples constantly. Verify everything before it ships.

Hooks. The best hooks come from a specific surprise or a moment from your own experience. AI doesn't know what happened on your last customer call.

## The mistakes that quietly kill most YouTube scripts

A checklist without context is just a chore, so instead of listing ten one-liners, here are the mistakes I see most often, why they kill retention, and what to do about each one.

**The hook explains what the video is about.** The viewer already knows what the video is about; they read the title. A hook that says "today we're going to talk about how to…" is a signal to viewers that the next 30 seconds will repeat the title. Make the hook a claim, a question, or a moment, not a summary.

**The intro is longer than the hook.** If your hook is 10 seconds and your intro runs 60, viewers will leave somewhere in those 60 seconds. The intro should be shorter than the hook. Two or three sentences is plenty.

**You haven't read the script aloud.** The single highest-leverage fix on this list. Half of any script changes the moment you read it out loud at speaking pace.

**Sentences run longer than one breath.** Long sentences look fine on the page and sound terrible on camera. Split anything that takes more than five seconds to say.

**No pattern interrupts marked.** Without them, the editor has nothing to work with and the visual track flatlines. If you're the editor, you'll regret the omission at midnight on a Tuesday.

**The CTA asks for three things.** Three CTAs equals zero conversions. Pick one. Make it specific.

**The outro runs longer than 15 seconds.** Long outros bleed watch time. A sign-off line is enough.

**The word count doesn't match the target length.** 160 to 180 words per minute. If your script is way over or under, the video will feel wrong before you shoot it.

**The script reads like writing, not speaking.** If you'd be embarrassed reading the script aloud to a friend in a coffee shop, rewrite it.

**The script is comprehensive instead of specific.** Comprehensive scripts lose to specific ones every time. If you can split your topic into a series of focused videos instead of one long one, do it.

A couple of alternatives to running a post-write checklist. Get a colleague who hasn't seen the script to read the first 200 words out loud and tell you what they think the video is about. If their answer is different from your one-sentence goal, the script isn't doing its job. Or record a 60-second voice memo of yourself reading the hook and the first body point, and listen back the next morning. Distance reveals problems that staring at a doc won't.

## Frequently asked questions

### How do I write a script for my YouTube video?

Start by writing the goal: the one thing the viewer should walk away with. Then write the hook. Then set up the expectation gap. Outline the body in bullets and order the points (second-best first, best second, third-best last). Build each point as a value loop (what, how, why). Mark pattern interrupts every 30 to 45 seconds. End with one specific CTA. Then read it out loud, time it, and trim.

### What are the 3 C's of script writing?

Clarity, conciseness, and conversational tone. Clarity means the viewer understands every sentence on first hearing. Conciseness means no word is wasted. Conversational means it sounds like something you'd actually say out loud. If a sentence fails any of the three, it's a candidate to cut.

### What is the 30-second rule on YouTube?

A rule of thumb that says you have roughly 30 seconds after the hook to convince a viewer to commit to the full video. YouTube itself treats the first 30 seconds as a discrete metric in Studio analytics, [called Intro retention](https://support.google.com/youtube/answer/9314415). Your intro and the start of your first section have to keep paying off what the hook promised.

### How many words should a 10-minute YouTube video script be?

Around 1,600 to 1,800 words at a natural speaking pace. If your script runs longer than 2,000 for a 10-minute target, you'll talk faster than feels natural or run long.

### Do YouTubers actually write scripts?

Many top YouTubers script at least the hook, the intro, and the CTA. Some script the whole video. The exceptions tend to be vloggers and live formats. If you've ever watched a tightly-edited channel and noticed there are no "ums" or wandering tangents, it's because there's a script. The naturalness is engineered.

### What's the best AI tool to write a YouTube script?

The right tool drafts fast and lets you rewrite for voice. ### How do I write a script for YouTube Shorts?

Hook in the first second, no introduction, payoff in 20 seconds, and a closing line that loops back to the hook so the algorithm replays the Short. Target 50 to 75 words total.

### Should I memorize my script or read it on camera?

Neither, completely. Memorise the hook word for word, internalise the structure and key phrases for the body, then talk to the camera rather than reciting. Reading on camera is obvious to viewers and kills retention. Memorising every word makes you stiff. Knowing the structure cold and improvising the connective tissue is what works for most creators I've seen.
