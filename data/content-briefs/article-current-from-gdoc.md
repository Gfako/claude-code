## How to write a YouTube video script in 9 steps

The order matters. Each step makes the next one easier. The most common reason scripts come out flat is people skip ahead to writing dialogue before they've understood what the video is actually doing.

### Step 1: Start with an idea you'd actually click on

Before I write a word, I finish this sentence:

***"After watching this video, the viewer will be able to, understand, or feel X."***

One thing. Not three. If I can't fill that in clearly, the video doesn't have a point yet, and no amount of clever scripting will save it.

There's a sequence I follow before I touch the script itself: idea, then rough title, then script. The idea is the single problem you're solving for the viewer or the single thing you're helping them get. The title is the click-through promise. You don't need a final, polished title before you start writing, but you need a working one. Without it, the script has no target to aim at, and you end up writing six different videos that share a topic.

The hardest part of this step is choosing what to leave out. Most teams want a video to do four things at once: explain the product, generate demand, address an objection, and welcome new viewers. A script that tries to do all four does none of them well. Pick the one job that matters most for this specific video and let the others wait for the next one.

**Takeaway:** Write a one-sentence goal for the video before anything else. If you can't say in one sentence what the viewer should walk away with, don't write the script yet.

### Step 2: Write your hook before anything else

**The first 15 seconds decide whether the rest of the script matters.** YouTube treats the first 30 seconds as its own metric in Studio analytics, called [Intro retention](https://support.google.com/youtube/answer/9314415), and YouTube's own [Creator Playbook](https://blog.youtube/creator-and-artist-stories/youtube-creator-playbook-tips-first-15/) names the first 15 seconds as the discrete "hook" window. By the time most people finish polishing their conclusion, they've already lost half their audience to a hook they wrote last.

A good example to study is *this video I created a while back: the [Synthesia "How to make AI videos in 5 minutes" video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VY0HP6H9AY0).*

In the first 15 seconds, the viewer gets three things:

- a clear outcome ("turn any idea into an engaging video"),  
- an objection removed ("you don't need any video editing experience"),  
- and a credibility signal ("used by the majority of Fortune 100 companies"). The intro itself was generated with an [AI avatar](https://www.synthesia.io/features/avatars), which doubles as a live preview of what the viewer will be able to make if they keep watching.

The hook and the product demo are the same 15 seconds.

That kind of compression is what a hook is supposed to do. State the outcome, kill the main objection, prove you're worth listening to, and ideally show the thing working before you ask the viewer to commit.

So I write the hook first. Before the outline. Before I know what the rest of the video will say.

Two hook formats work for me almost every time:

- **The promise hook.** Tell the viewer exactly what they'll get and why it matters now. *"In the next five minutes, I'll show you the YouTube script structure that doubled our channel's watch time."*  
- **The in-media-res hook.** "In media res" is a screenwriting term that just means "starting in the middle of the action." You drop the viewer into the most interesting moment of the video, then loop back. *"This is the slide that nearly cost us the deal. Here's what we changed."*

The thing nobody talks about: both hooks need to leave something unresolved. A teased reveal. A question the viewer can't answer yet. If your hook fully describes the video, you've written a headline, and headlines don't hold attention. They earn the click and then disappear.

I use the in-media-res format for stories with a single dramatic beat, and promise hooks for everything else. The promise hook is easier to land. Start there.

**Takeaway:** Write the hook first, before any other part of the script. The strongest hooks state an outcome, remove an objection, and prove you're worth listening to, all inside 15 seconds.

### Step 3: Set up the gap between what the viewer expects and what you'll deliver

Once the hook is written, the next job is to set up a small piece of psychology that keeps the viewer watching: the gap between what they expect and what they get.

People keep watching when reality beats their expectations, even slightly. They drop off when reality matches what they already assumed. Your script needs to plant the expectation, then beat it. That happens in the first minute and continues through every section.

A concrete way to do this: in the 30 seconds after your hook, name the common-sense answer most people would give to your question, then promise a different answer.

*"Most teams try to fix retention by editing harder. That doesn't work. The fix is upstream, in the script."*

You've now committed to a counter-intuitive payoff, and the viewer has a reason to stay until you deliver it.

This is also how you avoid the "first body point falls flat" problem. If your first real point is something the viewer has already heard, retention drops fast. The first point needs to be new, sharp, or framed in a way they haven't encountered.

**Takeaway:** Inside the first minute, plant an expectation about how the topic is usually handled, then promise something different. That gap is what keeps the viewer watching.

### Step 4: Outline in bullet points, then order them so the second-best point goes first

Once the hook and the setup are in place, I list every section of the video as a single bullet. No full sentences yet. Using the Synthesia tutorial above as a worked example, the outline for that [tutorial-style video](https://www.synthesia.io/tools/tutorial-video-maker) looked roughly like this:

- Hook (turn any idea into an engaging video in minutes, no editing skills)  
- Start with the Assistant (open dashboard, add a prompt or file, pick a template)  
- Customize the AI avatar (costume, location, actions)  
- Edit the script (manually or with prompts) and turn the video interactive  
- Add screen recordings and collaborate with the team  
- Translate the video into 140+ languages with lip sync  
- CTA and outro (next tutorial: create your own AI avatar clone)

Now the part most guides miss.

**The order of your body points matters more than you think.**

A counterintuitive rule that works on almost every video: **lead with your second-best point**, then your best, then the third best, and so on.

Most creators want to lead with their strongest point because they're worried viewers will leave before they hear it.

The opposite happens. If you open with your absolute best material and the next point is weaker, viewers feel the drop and drift away. If you open with a strong-but-not-strongest point, then deliver something even better, you create a pattern of "it keeps getting better" that keeps people watching to see what's next. It's the same reason great albums almost never lead with their best song.

If your outline already feels boring on paper, the video will be boring on camera. I rearrange bullets until I'd watch it myself. This is the cheapest possible round of editing.

Moving a bullet costs ten seconds.

Moving a paragraph costs ten minutes.

Re-shooting a section costs a day.

**Takeaway:** Lead the body with your second-best point so the viewer feels the video getting stronger. Save the best for two-thirds in.

### Step 5: Build each body point as a value loop

For each point in the body, give the viewer three things in this order:

1. **What it is.** Explain the point as simply as you can. One sentence is ideal.  
2. **How to do it.** Show the application. A specific example, a tactic, a real situation.  
3. **Why it matters.** Tie it back to the outcome the viewer came for. What changes if they apply this?

Most YouTube videos do step 1 and stop. That's why they feel thin. The "how" and the "why" are what move a point from "interesting" to "useful," and useful is what keeps the viewer watching.

A pattern I look for when I'm reviewing my own scripts: every body section should zoom out and zoom in. Start big picture, get tactical, return to big picture. The rhythm of going wide and then narrow is what makes a 10-minute video feel like 4\. Most flat videos are stuck at one altitude the whole way through.

**Takeaway:** Every body point should do three things: explain what it is, show how to apply it, and say why it matters.

### Step 6: Write the dialogue the way you'd actually speak it

**Writing for video is closer to writing a podcast than writing an essay.** Three rules I follow every time:

- **Use contractions.** "You'll," not "you will." "Don't," not "do not." Anyone who says "do not" out loud sounds like a robot. Anyone who says "I would never" sounds like a person.  
- **Keep sentences short enough to say in one breath.** If you can't, split them.  
- **Read it out loud as you write.** Not at the end. While you're writing. The second you stop reading aloud, your sentences will get longer.

I keep a personal blacklist of words that show up when I'm half-asleep at the keyboard: "furthermore," "moreover," "in conclusion," "as we all know," "in today's fast-paced world." If I catch any of these in a draft, I assume my brain went on autopilot and I rewrite the paragraph from scratch.

A trick I've stolen from podcast hosts: read the script aloud to an empty room. If you'd be embarrassed if a friend walked in mid-sentence, the line is too formal. Rewrite it.

**Takeaway:** If you can't say it out loud without stumbling, the viewer can't follow it. Speak the script as you write.

### Step 7: Mark pattern interrupts every 30 to 45 seconds

A pattern interrupt is anything that breaks the visual or audio rhythm: a cut to a different angle, a graphic, a sound effect, a tone shift, a quick aside to camera. The viewer's attention rebuilds every time the pattern shifts.

I mark them inline in the script with short notes in italics: *cut to b-roll of customer office, graphic showing the retention curve overlay, aside to camera saying "and yes, I tried this myself."*

For a 10-minute video, that's 13 to 20 pattern interrupts. Sounds like a lot. Watch any high-retention channel with the markers in mind and you'll see they're doing more than that. MrBeast averages roughly one interrupt every 2 seconds. You don't have to go that hard. "Every 30 seconds" is the floor, not the goal.

A common mistake: marking one interrupt per section and assuming the editing will fill in the rest. It won't. If you're working with an editor, they'll cut exactly what you wrote, no more. If you're your own editor (which most B2B creators are these days), past-you needs to leave future-you something to work with. The temptation in the timeline is always to add cuts later, but if they're not in the script, they're rarely in the final.

Either way: over-mark. If you're using something like [Synthesia's AI video editor](https://www.synthesia.io/tools/ai-video-editor) for the edit, it's easy to leave a marked interrupt in or take it out at the timeline level. The cost of one extra marker is zero. The cost of a flat 30-second stretch is a chunk of your retention curve.

**Takeaway:** Mark visual or tonal interrupts every 30 to 45 seconds at minimum. Over-mark rather than under-mark, whether you have an editor or you are one.

### Step 8: End with a CTA that feels native, not pushy

**The CTA is what you want the viewer to do next. One ask. Not three.** "Subscribe" *or* "watch this next video" *or* "comment with your worst script story." Pick one and make it specific. Three CTAs in 30 seconds gets you zero conversions.

The strongest CTAs feel like a natural next step from something the video has already discussed, not a separate ask bolted on at the end. If the body of your video has been talking about how hard it is to write good scripts at scale, a soft mention of a template or tool that solves that exact problem reads as helpful, not promotional. If you swerve to a CTA that isn't tied to anything you just covered, viewers can feel it and check out.

The outro should be short. A sign-off line, a thank-you, done. Long outros are where viewers leave before [the algorithm counts the watch as complete](https://www.searchenginewatch.com/2012/10/22/youtube-algorithm-change-time-watched-key-to-higher-video-search-rankings/). Some channels run outros that last 45 seconds. They're bleeding watch time and don't know it.

**Takeaway:** One ask, tied to a problem the video already established, delivered in under 30 seconds. Keep the outro under 10\.

### Step 9: Read it aloud, time it, and trim

I read the whole script out loud at a natural pace. Anywhere I stumble, I simplify. Anywhere I get bored, I cut. Then I time the read.

[At a natural pace, **one minute of video is roughly 160 to 180 words.**](https://www.isca-archive.org/interspeech_2006/yuan06_interspeech.html) If you wrote a 1,500-word script for a five-minute video, you're either going to talk too fast or run long. Either way, trim before recording. Trimming in post is twice the work and four times the pain.

The honest truth: my first drafts are always too long. I now write to 110% of target length on purpose, so I can cut to the bone in the final pass. The video gets better every time I shorten it.

**Takeaway:** Read aloud, time the read, cut anything you stumble on. The version that ships should be tighter than the version you finished.

## A YouTube script template you can copy

Use this as a starting structure. Fill in the brackets and rewrite in your voice.

*Video title.*

*Target length: X minutes.*

*Goal: after watching, the viewer will X.*

*Hook (0:00 to 0:15).* One sentence that tells the viewer what they'll get, or drops them into the most interesting moment of the video. Then one sentence that gives them a reason to keep watching, usually a teased reveal or a question.

*Setup (0:15 to 0:45).* Briefly say what most people get wrong about the topic, then promise a different angle. This is where you create the expectation gap. Two or three sentences, no more.

*Body.* Section by section, each one structured the same way: name the point, show how to apply it, say why it matters. Mark a pattern interrupt every 30 to 45 seconds in italics. Order your points so the second-best comes first, then the best, then the third best, and so on.

*CTA (last 30 seconds).* One specific ask, ideally tied to a problem the video has already raised.

*Outro (last 10 seconds).* A short sign-off. Done.

## How long should a YouTube video script be?

It depends on the format. Explainers and educational videos tend to land best between 5 and 10 minutes, brand and product videos between 90 seconds and 4 minutes, and Shorts under 60 seconds. Every second has to earn its place.

Once you know the target length, work backward to a word count. A natural speaking pace is [roughly 160 to 180 words per minute](https://www.isca-archive.org/interspeech_2006/yuan06_interspeech.html), which accounts for pauses and B-roll. So a 2-minute video is 320 to 360 words, a 5-minute video is 800 to 900, a 10-minute video is 1,600 to 1,800, and a 15-minute video is 2,400 to 2,700.

These are upper bounds. My rule: write to 110% of the target on purpose, then cut 10% in the read-aloud pass. The cuts almost always make the video stronger.

## A full YouTube script example

Here's a complete annotated 90-second script that runs end to end: hook, body, CTA, outro. The point is to show the value loop, the point ordering rule, and a native CTA all working together inside one script.

*Title: "The script change that doubled our YouTube watch time."*

*Goal: marketing teams understand the one structural fix that lifts retention.*

*Hook (0:00 to 0:10).* "Last year we re-recorded the same product video three times. Same script. Same presenter. Same shots. Watch time barely moved. Then we changed one structural thing and our average watch time doubled."

*Cut to a side-by-side retention curve, before and after.*

*Setup (0:10 to 0:25).* "Most teams try to lift retention by editing harder. Faster cuts, more graphics, tighter sound design. None of that worked for us. The fix wasn't in the edit. It was in the script, in the order of the body."

*Body (0:25 to 1:10).*

*Section 1, second-best point.* "We used to open the body with our strongest argument, because we were worried viewers would leave before they heard it. Turns out that's the worst thing you can do. Lead with your second-best point and you create a feeling of momentum. The viewer feels the video getting stronger."

*Cut to a graphic of three bars in ascending order.*

*Section 2, the best point.* "Then put the best argument in the middle. Not the start, not the end. Right where the viewer is committed but still curious. That's where a great point pays the biggest dividend in watch time."

*Aside to camera: "We tested this on six videos in a row. Same effect every time."*

*Section 3, the third-best point.* "And here's the part no one tells you: the third best point goes last in the body, not the second strongest. The progression we tested that worked best was second, first, fourth, third."

*CTA (1:10 to 1:25).* "If you want a template that has this body order baked in, we keep a working version in our [free script template library](https://www.synthesia.io/post/free-video-script-templates). It's the same one we use internally."

*Outro (1:25 to 1:30).* "That's the fix. See you next week."

Things to notice in this example:

- **The hook makes a specific, falsifiable claim** ("watch time doubled") and pays it off immediately with a visual.  
- **The setup names the common-sense answer ("edit harder")** and rejects it, opening an expectation gap.  
- **Each body point follows the value loop:** what the rule is, how to apply it, why it works.  
- **The CTA references a problem the body already raised** (body order is hard), so the link to the template lands as helpful instead of pushy.  
- **The outro is 5 seconds.** Any longer and you're paying for it in watch time.

## How do you write a script for YouTube Shorts?

**A Shorts script is not a shorter long-form script.** It's a completely different format, and shortened long-form scripts almost always get buried by the algorithm.

Three structural rules, in order of importance:

- **Hook in the first 1 to 3 seconds.** Not 15\. Vertical viewers swipe instantly. The first sentence has to land before the camera finishes settling.  
- **No introduction.** No "hey everyone," no name, no explanation of what the Short is about. The Short is the value. Start with it.  
- **Loop the ending.** End on a sentence that makes the first sentence make more sense, so the algorithm replays it. Replays count as watch time, and watch time is what the Shorts algorithm rewards.

A Shorts template, written plainly: open with one sentence that creates an information gap, deliver the payoff in two or three short beats over the next 20 seconds (with on-screen text reinforcing the key phrases), and close with a line that loops back to the opener.

**Target script length:** 50 to 75 words. Anything longer and you'll talk too fast on camera.

## YouTube script formats and when to use each one

There are a handful of script formats in common use. Most YouTube videos don't need anything fancy, but knowing the options helps you pick the right one for the kind of video you're making.

- **Bulleted outline plus dialogue.** A simple list of sections with the spoken lines written under each. This is the format I use for 95% of videos. Fast to write, easy to edit, easy to share with a stakeholder. Best for explainers, talking-head videos, founder messages, product demos.  
- **AV (Audio/Visual).** Two columns. Audio and dialogue on one side, what's on screen on the other. Best for interviews, sit-down product reviews, and any video where the visual track and the audio track are doing different things.  
- **VAG (Visual/Audio/Graphics).** Same as AV with a third column for graphics. Best for training videos and educational explainers with heavy use of charts, on-screen text, or overlays. Useful when a designer is going to be working from the script directly.  
- **Sequential Flow.** A single running document in chronological order, with action lines, dialogue, and direction all interleaved. Best for high-production work, short films, narrative-driven videos, and anything with scene work or multiple locations.  
- **Voiceover script.** Just the dialogue, written as narration, with timestamps and visual cues noted in italics. Best for B-roll-heavy videos where the speaker is never on camera. Common for product launches, recap videos, and brand films.  
- **Interview script.** Question list plus optional cue lines. Best for podcasts, founder interviews, and customer story videos. The script is a guide, not a wall.  
- **Shorts script.** Hook, payoff, loop. Short, vertical-first, no introduction. Best for repurposing long-form into clipped pieces or for native Shorts content.

If you're shooting alone or with a small team and you're not sure where to start, use the bulleted outline plus dialogue. It's the fastest to write and it transfers well to any of the other formats if you grow into them.

## Can you use AI to write YouTube scripts without sounding generic?

**Yes, if you treat AI as a draft engine, not a writer.** When teams let AI write end to end, the result reads like AI. When they use AI for the parts that benefit from speed and bring their own voice to the rest, it works.

Here's where I actually use it now.

**Where AI helps:**

- **Outlining.** I give the tool my goal and topic and ask for 5 to 10 angle options. Maybe one is interesting. I take it and rewrite. The other nine are useful as proof that the obvious angles are obvious.  
- **Hook variations.** I ask for 10 alternate hooks for my topic. One is usually salvageable. I rewrite it in my voice.  
- **First drafts of sections I'll heavily edit.** AI is fastest at generating "okay" first drafts. I'd rather rewrite an okay draft than stare at a blank page.  
- **Cutting.** I paste my draft and ask which three sentences add the least value. Surprisingly useful, since I'm too attached to my own writing to see this clearly.

**Where AI hurts:**

- **Voice.** Anything written end to end by AI sounds generic. Viewers can tell within a sentence. The current generation of models all share the same tells, and audiences are getting better at spotting them by the month.  
- **Specifics.** AI invents stats, quotes, and customer examples. Verify everything before it ships.  
- **Hooks.** The best hooks come from your own experience or a specific surprise. AI doesn't know what happened in your last customer call.

## The mistakes that quietly kill most YouTube scripts

A checklist is useful, but a checklist without context is just a chore. So instead of a flat list, here are the mistakes I see most often, why they kill retention, and what to do instead.

**Mistake 1: The hook explains what the video is about.**

The viewer already knows what the video is about, they read the title. A hook that says "today we're going to talk about how to..." is a signal that the script is going to repeat the title for the next 30 seconds. Fix it by making the hook a claim, a question, or a moment, not a summary. If your hook could be cut without losing information, it's not a hook.

**Mistake 2: The intro is longer than the hook.**

If your hook is 10 seconds and your intro is 60, viewers are going to leave somewhere in those 60 seconds. The intro should be shorter than the hook, not longer. Two or three sentences is plenty.

**Mistake 3: You haven't read the script aloud.**

This is the single highest-leverage fix on this list. If you haven't read your script out loud at speaking pace, you don't know how it actually sounds. Half the script will change as soon as you do.

**Mistake 4: Sentences run longer than one breath.**

Long sentences read fine on the page and sound terrible on camera. The simplest fix is to split anything that takes more than five seconds to say.

**Mistake 5: No pattern interrupts marked.**

Without interrupts, the editor has nothing to work with and the visual track flatlines. Over-mark rather than under-mark.

**Mistake 6: The CTA asks for three things.**

Three CTAs equals zero conversions. Pick one ask. Make it specific. Tie it to something the video has already covered.

**Mistake 7: The outro runs longer than 15 seconds.**

Long outros bleed watch time. A sign-off line is enough.

**Mistake 8: The word count doesn't match the target length.**

160 to 180 words per minute. If your script is way over or under, the video is going to feel wrong before you even shoot it.

**Mistake 9: The script reads like writing, not speaking.**

If you'd be embarrassed reading the script aloud to a friend in a coffee shop, rewrite it.

**Mistake 10: The script is comprehensive instead of specific.**

Comprehensive scripts lose to specific ones every time. If you can split your topic into a series of focused videos instead of one long one, do it.

A few alternatives to running a checklist after the fact: have a colleague who hasn't seen the script read the first 200 words aloud and ask what they think the video is about. If their answer is different from your one-sentence goal, the script isn't doing its job yet. Or record a 60-second voice memo of yourself reading the hook and the first body point, then listen back the next morning. Distance reveals problems that staring at a doc won't.

## Frequently asked questions

### How do I write a script for my YouTube video?

Start by writing the **goal**, the one thing the viewer should walk away with. Write the **hook** before anything else. Then set up the expectation gap, outline the body in bullets, order your points (second-best first, best second, third-best last), structure each point as a value loop (what, how, why), mark pattern interrupts every 30 to 45 seconds, and end with one specific CTA. Read it out loud, time it, and trim. The order matters more than people realise.

### What are the 3 C's of script writing?

**Clarity, conciseness, and conversational tone.** Clarity means the viewer understands every sentence on first hearing. Conciseness means no word is wasted. Conversational means it sounds like something you'd actually say out loud. All three serve the same goal: keep the viewer watching. If a sentence fails any of the three, it's a candidate to cut.

### What is the 30-second rule on YouTube?

It's a rule of thumb that says you have roughly **30 seconds after the hook to convince a viewer to commit to the full video.** YouTube treats the first 30 seconds as a discrete metric in Studio analytics, called [Intro retention](https://support.google.com/youtube/answer/9314415). Your intro and the start of your first section have to keep paying off what the hook promised, or that 30-second mark is where you lose them.

### How many words should a 10-minute YouTube video script be?

**Around 1,600 to 1,800 words** at a natural speaking pace. If your script runs longer than 2,000 words for a 10-minute target, you'll either talk faster than feels natural or your finished video will run long. Trim before you record. Trimming after is two extra hours in the edit.

### Do YouTubers actually write scripts?

Many top YouTubers script at least the hook, the intro, and the CTA. Some script the whole video. The exceptions tend to be vloggers and live formats. If you've ever watched a tightly edited channel and noticed there are no "ums" or wandering tangents, it's because there's a script. The "naturalness" is engineered.

### How do I write a script for YouTube Shorts?

**Hook in the first second, no introduction, payoff in 20 seconds, and end on a line that loops back to the hook** so the algorithm replays it. Target 50 to 75 words total. Anything longer and you're talking too fast on camera.

### Should I memorize my script or read it on camera?

Neither, fully. **Memorize the hook word for word. Internalize the structure and key phrases for the body, then talk to the camera rather than reciting.** Reading is obvious to viewers and kills retention. Memorizing word for word makes you stiff. Knowing the structure cold and improvising the connective tissue is the format that works for me and most of the creators I've worked with.
