## C. AI Pattern Analysis

```
AI Pattern Instances Found: 18+
Severity: High (9+ instances)

Structural issues: 14 — Every strategy block uses identical format (H3 → paragraph → Example → Start here)
Phrasing issues: 4+ — Generic claims without evidence, vague filler phrases
Tone issues: Uniformly neutral; personality only appears in "From experience" callout

Highest-risk sections:
1. Strategies 1-14 (identical template repeated 14 times)
2. Strategy 2 ("creates a more dynamic experience, capture attention")
3. Strategy 4 ("lands with greater clarity and impact")
```

### Structural patterns

**14 identical strategy blocks:** This is the single biggest AI-detection risk. Every strategy follows:
1. `### N) Title`
2. One explanation paragraph
3. `**Example:**` one sentence
4. `**▶️ Start here:**` one sentence

This rigid repetition across 14 sections is a textbook AI pattern. Human writers naturally vary format — some sections would be longer, some would use a different structure, some would skip the example or lead with it.

**Suggested fix:** Vary at least 5-6 of the 14 blocks. Options:
- Lead with the example instead of the explanation
- Use a mini case study (2-3 sentences with a named company or team)
- Use a before/after comparison
- Drop the "Start here" CTA on some blocks and use a different closing device
- Merge thin strategies (5, 6, 8) into a single section with more depth

### Phrasing patterns

Flagged instances:
- "create a more dynamic experience, capture attention, and communicate tone and context more effectively" (strategy 2) — triple-stacked generic claims
- "lands with greater clarity and impact" (strategy 4) — vague superlative
- "Making a choice creates investment in the outcome" (strategy 4) — sounds like a motivational poster
- "strengthens understanding and supports recall later" (strategy 8) — generic
- "the learning becomes more practical and durable" (strategy 9) — vague

Em dashes: ~6-7 across ~2,500 words = ~2.5 per 1,000 words. Within acceptable range.

Formulaic transitions: Minimal. The article avoids "Moreover," "Furthermore," etc. This is good.

### Tone patterns

The article is uniformly professional-neutral throughout the strategy blocks. The only moment of genuine personality is the "From experience" callout. No opinions, no humor, no surprise, no "I've seen teams get this wrong because..." moments. The contrast between the callout (alive, specific, human) and the strategies (flat, templated, interchangeable) makes the AI patterns in the strategies even more visible.

### Rewrite guidance for highest-risk sections

**Strategy 2 (current):**
> Training delivered through slides, PDFs, or long documents often feels passive and easy to ignore. Training videos create a more dynamic experience, capture attention, and communicate tone and context more effectively than text alone.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> I've sat through enough 40-slide policy decks to know that most people stop reading after slide 3. The problem isn't the content — it's the format. Video lets you demonstrate a procedure in 90 seconds instead of describing it across 5 pages, and it carries tone that text can't. A written "handle with care" doesn't land the same way as watching someone walk through the consequences of getting it wrong.

**Strategy 4 (current):**
> When learners commit to an answer first, the guidance that follows lands with greater clarity and impact. Making a choice creates investment in the outcome.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> There's a reason game shows make contestants lock in their answer before revealing the correct one. Once you've committed to a choice, you pay closer attention to the explanation — you want to know if you were right and why. In training, this means putting the question before the lesson, not after it.
