Most of the YouTube script feedback I give comes down to the handful of problems.

* The intro runs long.

* The hook describes the video of selling it.

* The middle wanders.

* The call to action tries to do three things at

* And nobody can tell whether they're stuck on the script or on the delivery

so the next video gets re-shot when it should have been re-written.

I've been writing scripts for the Synthesia channel for a years now.

The longer I do it the more convinced I am that the script does work than anything else in the production.

Cameras are important.

Lighting is important.

The editing software you use also matters.

The first 200 words of the script matter more than all of them combined.

This post is the process I actually use, broken into nine steps.

Not the polished six-point version you'll see on blogs.

Some of the steps sound obvious.

## How to write a YouTube video script in 9 steps

The sequence matters more than people realise.

Each step is meant to set up the one.

The single common reason scripts come out flat:

people skip ahead to writing dialogue before they've worked out what the video is for.

### Step 1: Start with an idea you'd actually click on

Before I write a single word I make myself finish this sentence:

"After watching this video the viewer will be able to understand or feel X."

One thing only.

If I can't fill that in clearly the video doesn't have a point yet

No amount of clever writing later will save it.

After the idea I rough out a working title.

Not the final polished version

(titles change in editing all the time)

Something close enough to give the script a target.

I learned the way that if you start writing without a title

you end up writing about three different videos that share a topic.

The part most teams struggle with here is choosing what to leave out.

Brand teams in particular want a video to

* explain the product,

* generate demand,

* address an objection,

* and welcome new viewers.

Pick one.

The others will get their videos.

A script that tries to serve four masters serves none of them.

To make this concrete:

here's an example of an idea that did the work for me before I wrote anything

"How to translate any video into any language with lip sync."

Translation and localization were already at the top of every customers wishlist

so the idea had built-in pull before I touched the script.

I supported it with a stat that framed the size of the problem

(70% of business content online is in English and about 19% of people speak it).

That's a sentence and it does an enormous amount of work.

The takeaway buried in that example is something I wish someone had told me three years ago:

a sharp idea does some of the persuasion before the thumbnail, title or hook get a turn.

Hooks are amplifiers.

Thumbnails are amplifiers.

The idea is the signal they're amplifying.

If the signal is weak no amount of amplification will help.

Takeaway:

Write a one-sentence goal for the video before anything

If you can't say it cleanly in one breath don't move to the script yet.

### Step 2: Write your hook before anything

The first 15 seconds decide whether the rest of the script matters.

YouTube treats the 30 seconds as its own retention metric in Studio analytics

(they call it Intro retention)

and the official YouTube Creator Playbook explicitly names the first 15 seconds as the hook window.

A good example to study, if you'll forgive the self-reference

is the Synthesia "How to make AI videos in 5 minutes" video.

In those 15 seconds the viewer gets three things at once.

* A clear outcome

(turn any idea into a video).

* An objection removed

(you don't need video editing skills).

* A credibility signal

(used by most of the Fortune 100).

The intro itself was generated with an AI avatar using Synthesias script-to-video workflow,

which means the hook is the product demo.

The viewer doesn't have to imagine what the output looks like; they're watching it.

That kind of compression is what a good hook does.

It states the outcome

kills the objection

proves you're worth listening to

and shows the thing working

all before asking the viewer to commit to a few more minutes.

I write the hook before anything

Before the outline.

Before I've decided what the body will cover.

Two formats keep working for me:

* The promise hook

tells the viewer what they're about to get and why now.

Something like,

"In the five minutes I'll show you the script structure that doubled our channels watch time."

* The in-media-res hook

is a screenwriting term that just means starting in the middle of the action.

You drop the viewer into the interesting moment of the video

then loop back to set it up.

"This is the slide that nearly cost us the deal.

Here's what we changed."

It's a technique

(Homer used it)

and it still works because curiosity is older than YouTube.

Whichever format you use

the hook has to leave something

A teased reveal,

a question without an answer,

a claim that needs proof.

If your hook fully describes the video

it's a headline.

Headlines earn the click. Then disappear.

A hook holds the viewer.

The promise hook is easier to land on the try

so start there.

The, in-media-res version is leverage when it works

but the reveal has to actually pay off;

if it doesn't

you'll lose viewers faster than a flat hook would have.

Takeaway:

Write the hook first.

The strongest hooks state an outcome

remove an objection

prove you're worth listening to

Leave something unresolved.

Step 3: Set up the gap between what people expect and what you will deliver
Once you have written the hook the next thing to do is to add a piece of psychology that keeps the viewer watching: the gap between what they expect and what you are going to deliver.
People keep watching when what actually happens is better than what they expected even if it is a little bit. They stop watching when what actually happens is what they thought it would be. So the script needs to set up an expectation and then beat it.
In practice this means that in the 30 seconds after your hook you should say what the common sense answer is and then promise something. For example you could say "Most teams try to fix retention by editing. That does not work. The fix is actually in the script." Now the viewer knows you are going to say something from what they expected and they will keep watching at least until you prove it.
This is also how you avoid having a point that is not very interesting. If your first real argument is something the viewer has heard before they will stop watching. The first point of the body has to be either new or sharper than peoples version or framed in a way the viewer has not seen before.
Takeaway: Inside the minute say what the common sense answer is and then promise something different. The gap between what people expect and what you deliver is what keeps the viewer watching.
Step 4: Outline in bullet points then order them so the second best point goes first
Once the hook and the setup are in place make a list of every section of the video as a bullet point. Just the bullet points, not sentences yet. For example if you were making a video about the Synthesia tutorial the outline might look like this:
* Hook
* Start with the Assistant
* Customise your AI avatar
* Edit the script. Turn the video interactive
* Add screen. Let the team collaborate
* Translate the video into languages with lip sync
* Call to action and outro
Now most guides do not tell you that the order of your body points matters a lot.
The rule that surprises people is: lead with your best point, not your strongest. Then your best point. Then your third best point.. So on.
The instinct most creators have is to lead with the argument because they are worried viewers will leave before they hear it.. What actually happens is the opposite. If you start with the material the next point will feel weaker by comparison and viewers will feel the drop and stop watching. If you start with a not strongest point and then deliver something noticeably better you create a pattern of "this keeps getting better" that keeps people watching to see what is next.
If the outline already feels boring on paper the finished video will be boring on camera. Move things around until you would watch it yourself. It is easy to move a bullet point. It is hard to move a paragraph or reshoot a section. So edit ruthlessly while it is still easy.
Takeaway: Lead the body with your best point so the viewer feels the video getting stronger. Save the best for two thirds in.
Step 5: Build each body point as a value loop
For each point in the body give the viewer three things in this order:
What it is. Explain the point simply as you can.
How to do it. Show the application: an example, a tactic, a real situation.
Why it matters. Connect it back to the outcome the viewer came for.
Youtube videos do the first step and stop which is why they feel thin. The "how" and the "why" are what move a point from interesting to useful and useful is what gets people to keep watching.
One more pattern to add here is: every body section should zoom out and zoom in. Big picture, tactical then back to big picture. The rhythm of going then narrow then wide again is what makes a 10 minute video feel like four.
Takeaway: Every body point should explain what it is show how to apply it and tell the viewer why it matters.
Step 6: Write the dialogue the way you would actually speak it
Writing for video is much closer to writing a podcast than writing an essay.
First use contractions. Always. "You will" becomes "you'll". "Do not" becomes "don't". Nobody says "do not" loud in conversation so it sounds robotic when read aloud.
Second keep every sentence short to say in one breath. If you stumble on a sentence when reading it out loud split it.
Third read everything loud as you write. Not at the end as a check but while you are writing.
I have a list of phrases that show up when my brain is on autopilot: "furthermore" "moreover" "in conclusion". If I catch any of them in a draft I rewrite the paragraph.
A trick I learned from podcasters is: read the script aloud to a room. If you would be embarrassed if a friend walked in through and heard you the line is too formal. Rewrite it until it sounds like something you would actually say.
Takeaway: If you cannot say it loud without stumbling the viewer cannot follow it. Speak the script as you write.
Step 7: Mark pattern interrupts every 30 to 45 seconds
A pattern interrupt is anything that breaks the audio rhythm of the video.
I mark them inline in the script in italics. For example "cut to b roll of customer office" "graphic showing the retention curve overlay" "aside to camera saying 'and yes I tried this myself'".
For a 10 minute video that is 13 to 20 interrupts. It sounds like a lot until you actually watch a high retention channel back with the markers in mind.
If you are working with an editor they will cut exactly what is in the script no more. If you are your editor you need to leave yourself something to work with.
Takeaway: Mark interrupts every 30 to 45 seconds at minimum whether you have an editor or you are one. Over mark.
Step 8: End with a call to action that feels native not pushy
One ask. Not three. "Subscribe". Watch the next video" or "drop a comment with your worst script story". Pick one. Make it specific.
What makes a call to action actually land is whether it feels like a next step from something the video has been talking about. If the body has been about how hard it's to write good scripts at scale a soft mention of a template or tool that solves that exact problem reads as helpful.
Keep the outro short. A sign off a thank you gone. Long outros are where viewers leave before the algorithm registers the watch as complete.
Takeaway: One ask, tied to a problem the body already raised delivered in under 30 seconds. Outro under 10.
Step 9: Read it time it trim it
Read the whole script out loud at a natural pace. Wherever you stumble, simplify. Wherever you get bored cut. Then time the read.
At a pace one minute of finished video runs to roughly 160 to 180 words. If you wrote a 1,500 word script, for a five minute video you are going to talk fast or run long.
Trim before you record. Trimming in post is twice the work and post shoot you are emotionally attached to footage.
My honest experience is that the first drafts I write are always too long. I have found that I need to write a lot at first. Now I make sure to write a little more than I need to 110% of what I am aiming for. This way I can go back. Cut out the extra parts when I read it out loud. The more I cut the better the video gets.
The main thing I learned is to read my work loud. I time myself to see how long it takes. If I stumble on any part I cut it out. The final version should be shorter and better, than the version I wrote.

So how long should your YouTube video script be? Well it depends on the type of video you are making. If you are making an explainer video or an educational video it should be around 5 to 10 minutes long. If you are making a brand or product video it should be around 90 seconds to 4 minutes long. If you are making a video it should be under 60 seconds. The key is to make sure every second of your video is useful.
Once you know how long your video should be you can work backwards to figure out how words your script should be. A good rule of thumb is to assume a speaking pace of around 160 to 180 words per minute. This takes into account pauses and other things that will happen during your video. So a 2-minute video should be around 320 to 360 words a 5-minute video should be around 800 to 900 words a 10-minute video should be around 1,600 to 1,800 words and a 15-minute video should be around 2,400 to 2,700 words.
These are just upper limits. My rule of thumb is to write a script that's around 110% of the target length and then cut it back by around 10% when I read it out loud. I have found that cutting my script always makes it stronger.
Here is an example of a YouTube script.
The title of this video is "The script change that doubled our YouTube watch time."
The goal of this video is to help marketing teams understand how to make one change to their script that will increase the amount of time people spend watching their videos.
The hook is: " year we re-recorded the same product video three times. We used the script, the same presenter and the same shots.. No matter what we did we could not get people to watch the video for more than a few seconds. Then we made one change to the script and our average watch time doubled."
Then we cut to a graph that shows how our watch time changed.
The setup is: "Most teams try to increase watch time by editing their videos. They make cuts add more graphics and use tighter sound design.. None of that worked for us. We found that the problem was not with the editing it was with the script."
The body of the video is broken up into three sections.
The first section is: "We used to start our videos with our argument.. We found that this was actually the worst thing we could do. If you lead with your strongest point you create a sense of momentum. The viewer feels like the video is getting better and better."
Then we cut to a graphic that shows three bars in ascending order.
The second section is: "Then you put your argument in the middle. Not at the start not at the end. Right where the viewer is committed but still curious. That is where a great point pays off the most."
We also have an aside to the camera where we say: "We tested this on six videos in a row. It worked every time."
The third section is: "The third best point goes last. The progression that works best is second, first, fourth third."
The call to action is: "If you want a template that has this body order built in you can find it in our free script template library. It is the template we use internally."
The outro is: "That is the fix. See you week."
There are a things to notice about this script.
The hook makes a claim and pays it off right away with a visual.
The setup names the common sense answer. Rejects it which opens up an expectation gap.
Every point in the body follows the pattern: what the rule is, how to apply it and why it works.
The call to action references a problem that was raised in the body so it feels like a continuation of the video than a sales pitch.
The outro is very short.
So how do you write a script for a YouTube Short?
A Shorts script is not a shorter version of a long-form script. It is a format altogether. If you take a long-form script and just shorten it it will not work well.
There are three rules to keep in mind when writing a Shorts script.
The first rule is to hook the viewer in the one to three seconds. Not 15 seconds. Viewers on screens will swipe away instantly if you do not grab their attention right away.
The second rule is to not have an introduction. No "hey everyone," no name card, no explanation of what the Short's about. The Short is the value so you should start with it.
The third rule is to loop the ending. You should end with a sentence that makes the first sentence make sense so the algorithm will replay it. Replays count as watch time and watch time is what the Shorts algorithm rewards.
Here is a template for a Shorts script:
Open with one sentence that creates an information gap.
Deliver the payoff in two or three beats over the next 20 seconds. Use on-screen text to reinforce the phrases.
Close with a line that loops back to the opener.
Your script should be around 50 to 75 words long. If it is any longer you will talk fast on camera.
There are a few formats you can use for your YouTube script.
* Outline plus dialogue: this is a simple list of sections with the spoken lines underneath. It is fast to write, easy to share with stakeholders and easy to edit. It is best for explainers, talking-head videos, founder messages and product demos.
* AV (Audio/Visual): this format has two columns, one for audio and one for what's on screen. It is best for interviews sit-down product reviews and any video where the audio track and the visual track are doing things.
* VAG (Visual/Audio/Graphics): this format is like AV. It has a third column for graphics. It is best for training videos and educational explainers that use a lot of charts on-screen text or overlays.
* Sequential Flow: this format is a running document with action lines, dialogue and direction interleaved. It is best for high-production work, short films, narrative-driven videos and anything with scene work.
* Voiceover script: this format is dialogue written as narration with timestamps and visual cues noted in italics. It is best for B-roll- videos where the speaker is never on camera.
* Interview script: this format is a question list with cue lines. It is best for podcasts, founder interviews and customer story videos. The script is a guide, not a wall.
* Shorts script: this format is hook, payoff, loop as described above. It is best for repurposing long-form content into clipped pieces or for vertical content.
If you are shooting alone or with a team and you are not sure where to start you should default to the bulleted outline plus dialogue. It is the fastest to write, the easiest to refine. It transfers cleanly to any of the other formats once you grow into them.
Can you use AI to write YouTube scripts without sounding generic?
Yes,. Only if you treat AI as a draft engine and not as a writer. When teams let AI generate scripts the output sounds like it was written by AI. When they use AI for the parts that benefit from speed and bring their voice to the rest it works.
I use AI for a parts of the workflow.
I use it for outlining. I give the tool a goal and a topic. Ask for five to ten angle options. Maybe one of them is interesting. I take that. Rewrite it. The other nine are useful as proof that the obvious anglesre obvious.
I use it for hook variations. I ask for ten hooks for a topic. One of them is usually salvageable. I rewrite it in my voice.
I use it for drafts of sections I am planning to heavily edit anyway. AI is fastest at producing "first drafts and I would rather rewrite an okay draft than stare at a blank page.
I use it for cutting. I paste my draft. Ask which three sentences add the least value. This is surprisingly useful. I am too attached, to my writing to see this clearly.
The parts where AI consistently hurts are when it is used to write scripts. The output always sounds generic. Like it was written by a machine.
Voice is really important. When artificial intelligence writes something from start to finish it sounds very generic. Viewers can usually tell that it is not written by a human within the sentence. The current models of intelligence all have the same characteristics and audiences are getting better at recognizing them every month.
Specific details are also crucial. Artificial intelligence often makes up statistics, quotes and customer examples. You should always verify everything before you use it.
A good hook is essential. The best hooks come from a surprise or a moment from your experience. Artificial intelligence does not know what happened on your customer call so it cannot come up with a hook that is personal and relatable.

There are some mistakes that can ruin a YouTube script.
* The hook does not grab the viewers attention.
* The intro is too long.
* You have not read the script aloud.
* The sentences are too long.
* There are no pattern interrupts marked.
* The call to action is confusing.
* The outro is too long.
* The word count is not right.
* The script sounds like writing, not speaking.
* The script tries to cover much.
A good hook should be a claim, a question or a moment, not a summary. The viewer already knows what the video is about from the title so you do not need to repeat it in the hook. If your hook is ten seconds long your intro should be shorter than that. Two or three sentences is enough.
You should always read your script aloud before you record it. This is the important thing you can do to make sure your script is good. When you read it aloud you will notice that some things do not sound right and you can change them.
Sentences that are too long are hard to follow. If a sentence takes than five seconds to say you should break it up into two or more sentences.
You should also mark pattern interrupts in your script. These are places where you can add visuals or music to make the video more interesting. If you do not mark them the editor will not know where to add them. The visual track will be boring.
Your call to action should be simple and clear. If you ask viewers to do three things they will probably not do any of them. You should pick one thing. Make it specific.
Your outro should be short. If it is too long viewers will stop watching. A simple sign-off line is enough.
The word count of your script is also important. If your script is too long or too short the video will not feel right. You should aim for 160 to 180 words per minute.
Your script should sound like speaking not writing. If you would be embarrassed to read your script to a friend you should rewrite it.
It is better to have a script than a comprehensive one. If you can split your topic into focused videos you should do that.
Of using a checklist to review your script you can ask a colleague to read the first 200 words aloud and tell you what they think the video is about. If their answer is different from your goal you need to rewrite the script. Alternatively you can record yourself reading the hook and the first body point. Listen to it the next morning. Sometimes taking a break, from your script can help you see problems that you did not notice before.

* What are the 3 Cs of script writing?
The 3 Cs are clarity, conciseness and conversational tone.
* Clarity means your viewers understand every sentence the time
* Conciseness means no word is wasted
* Conversational tone means it sounds natural
If a sentence doesn't meet these criteria consider cutting it.
* What is the 30-second rule on YouTube?
You've got 30 seconds after the hook to convince viewers to watch your whole video. YouTube looks at the 30 seconds as a separate metric called Intro retention. Your intro and first section must deliver on what the hook promised.
* How words should a 10-minute YouTube video script be?
A 10-minute video script should be around 1,600 to 1,800 words. If your script is over 2,000 words you might talk fast or run long.
* Do YouTubers actually write scripts?
Many popular YouTubers write scripts for their hook intro and call-to-action. Some write scripts for their video. Exceptions include vloggers and live formats. If you notice a channel with editing and no filler words it's likely because they have a script.

* How do I write a script for YouTube Shorts?

* Pay off the hook in 20 seconds

* Should I memorize my script. Read it on camera?
Don't memorize or read your script word-for-word. Memorize your hook understand your structure and key points and then talk naturally to the camera. Reading on camera looks fake and memorizing makes you stiff.
