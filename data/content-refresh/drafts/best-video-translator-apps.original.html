Which AI video translation tool should you use?

Most AI video translation tools look great in demos. But when you actually test them, the lip-sync is off, the voice cloning sounds robotic, and the translation just isn't right.

I tested 10 tools head-to-head to find out which ones hold up, so you don't have to waste time or money finding out the hard way.

Below you'll find my honest breakdown of each tool, including what the dubbing quality is really like, how the pricing stacks up, and who each one makes sense for.

The best AI video translator tools at a glance
  • Synthesia: Best overall for business: highest-quality lip-sync, voice cloning, and enterprise-ready workflows.
  • AI Studios: Best free and budget option for lip-synced dubbing (up to 3 minutes free, cheapest per-minute rates).
  • Perso AI: Cheapest paid option for lip-synced dubbing, but you have to upgrade to download your video.
  • ElevenLabs: Best for voiceover-only dubbing with ultra-realistic voice cloning.
  • Maestra: Clunky interface and lip-sync is enterprise-only, but good value for large-volume translation projects.
  • Kapwing: Good if you want video editing and basic dubbing in one tool.
  • Speechify: Voiceover-only dubbing with limited control and inconsistent translation quality.
  • Murf: Generous free voiceover minutes, but no lip-sync and voice cloning is enterprise-only.
  • Veed: All-in-one editor, but no free plan and below-average dubbing quality.
  • Rask AI: Expensive, and the lip-sync quality is very poor.

How I tested AI video translation tools

To make this a fair test, I used every tool to translate the same short video of me speaking English into European Spanish. I tested both the free and paid versions of each tool where possible.

I assessed each AI video translator tool for the following criteria:

  • Accuracy: how accurate are the subtitles generated? How accurate is the dubbed speech? Does the translation sound natural to a native speaker?
  • Voice cloning: does the dubbed voice sound like the original person, or is it a robotic-sounding AI voice?
  • Lip-sync: does the video visually adjust to match the new dubbed voice?
  • Editing features: is there functionality to edit subtitles, scripts, and correct audio? (AI translations aren't always perfect)
  • Language support: How many languages can the tool translate into?
  • Ease of use: Is the interface intuitive and easy to learn?
  • Pricing: Is the service good value for money?
What are the different ways to translate a video?

When translating videos you have two options:

  1. Subtitles. Translating spoken content into written text displayed on-screen. Adding subtitles to videos allow viewers to read the translation while hearing the original audio. But not everyone loves reading and watching a video at the same time.
  2. Dubbing. Translating and replacing the original audio with a new track in the target language. This can be standard voiceover dubbing, where the video stays the same, or AI lip-sync dubbing, where the video is altered so the speaker’s mouth movements match the translated speech. Poor dubbing can be an unwanted distraction, so quality and accuracy is super important.

1. Synthesia

Pros

  • The highest quality lip-sync of any AI dubbing tool on this list
  • Voice cloning that sounds exactly like me
  • Wide language support (130+)
  • API and bulk workflows for translating videos at scale

Cons

  • Free version only lets you dub videos up to 1 minute long (but you can upload a longer video, it'll just be trimmed)

My experience

Here's the translated video using Synthesia (note: you can swap to the original English version to compare it in the Multilingual Video Player):

ES-ES - Dubbing Example (English) - Video generated with Synthesia

Synthesia offers free AI dubbing with lip-sync for videos up to 1 minute. You can upload .mp4, .mov, or .webm files, and you can also use it to translate a YouTube video.

I found it the easiest tool on this list to use. The lip-sync quality is the best I tested. Mouth movements look spot on, and the pacing, pauses, and emphasis are close enough to the original that it still feels like my video, just in another language.

The "Adaptive" video length option is a nice touch. It adjusts the video duration to match the natural speaking speed of the target language, which is especially useful for languages like German.

After dubbing, I could open the transcript editor and see the original script alongside the translated version, broken into timed segments. This is really useful in professional settings where terminology matters, such as product names, acronyms, or legal language. You can click into any segment and correct the wording before finalizing.

Pricing

Pricing accurate as of April 2026.

Free for videos up to 1 minute with full lip-sync and voice cloning. For longer videos, the Starter plan is $14/month billed annually and includes 10 minutes of AI dubbing at $1.40/minute. See full pricing details.

Who should use it?

Synthesia makes sense for anyone who needs high-quality AI video translation, but it's the strongest fit for business use cases. It's built for teams that need more than a one-off translation, e.g. L&D libraries, compliance training, product demos, and onboarding content that needs to work across multiple languages and markets. Features like SSO-protected video pages, brand kit enforcement, SCORM support, and bulk dubbing via API make it suitable for enterprise rollouts where quality, consistency, and governance all matter.

2. AI Studios

Pros

  • Most generous free plan for lip-synced dubbing (up to 3 minutes)
  • Offers both lip-sync and voiceover dubbing
  • Lowest per-minute rate for lip-synced dubbing on this list
  • Accurate translations
  • Supports 150+ languages

Cons

  • Lip-sync drifts in places, with some visible jittering
  • Voice cloning quality could be more accurate. It isn't at a professional grade

My experience

Here's the translated video using AI Studios:

AI_Studios - Video generated with Synthesia

AI Studios lets you do lip-synced dubbing on videos up to 3 minutes long on a free account. That's the most generous free allowance of any tool I tested.

The translation is accurate and the voice cloning sounds more or less like me. The lip-sync quality is reasonable for most use cases. I also found the post-dubbing editing features solid. You can refine the dubbed script to improve timing, flow, and accuracy without burning through credits on extra generations.

The main issue I ran into was lip-sync drift. In a few moments the sync slipped and there was some visible jittering. It's the kind of thing I wouldn't want in a polished business video, but for a quick social clip or informal use it's unlikely to matter much.

Pricing

Pricing accurate as of April 2026.

Free plan includes up to 3 minutes of AI dubbing per month. The Personal plan is $24/month and gives you 120 minutes of dubbing at $0.20/minute.

Who should use it?

AI Studios is the best option if you want lip-synced dubbing on a budget. The quality isn't quite professional grade, but if that's not a dealbreaker for your use case, the value is hard to beat.

3. Perso AI

Pros

  • Affordable lip-synced dubbing
  • Voice cloning that sounds close to the original

Cons

  • You can't download your translated video on the free plan
  • Timing and mismatch issues with the lip-syncing

My experience

Here's the translated video using Perso AI:

Perso_AI_LIPSYNC - Video generated with Synthesia

Perso AI's lip-sync quality is pretty good, but not at the level where I'd fully trust it for a high-stakes business video. There are small timing issues and subtle mismatches. These are fine for quick demos or social content, but a bit distracting if you're aiming for a very polished result.

The voice cloning is solid. In my test, the cloned voice sounded close enough to my real voice that it didn't feel like a generic AI narrator.

One limitation to flag: the free plan lets you generate a dubbed video but won't let you download it without upgrading. That makes the free tier more of a preview than something you can use end-to-end.

The editing features are useful. You can tweak the translated script and timing, correct wording, adjust pacing, and regenerate sections without starting from scratch. Perso AI supports 32+ languages, covering most common business and content use cases.

Pricing

Pricing accurate as of April 2026.

Free plan includes 1 minute of dubbing but no video export. The Creator plan is $29/month and gives you 30 minutes of lip-synced dubbing at $0.97/minute.

Who should use it?

If you're on a tight budget and need lip-sync, Perso AI is worth a look. That said, Synthesia and AI Studios both offer better dubbing quality and let you download on the free plan, so they're the better starting point for most people.

4. ElevenLabs

Pros

  • 5 minutes of free voiceover dubbing (watermarked)
  • Excellent voice cloning that sounds just like me
  • Very granular editing controls in the Dubbing Studio

Cons

  • Voiceover dubbing only, no lip-sync
  • Some unnatural intonations in the dubbed audio
  • Only supports 29 languages
  • Steeper learning curve than most tools on this list

My experience

Here's the translated video using ElevenLabs:

ElevenLabs_Spanish - Video generated with Synthesia

ElevenLabs is best known for its AI voice generation, and the Dubbing Studio is focused purely on high-quality voiceover translation, with no lip-sync option.

The voice cloning did a great job of preserving my tone and emotion. The translated audio still sounded like me rather than a generic AI voice. That said, I noticed a few strange moments. When I say "compruébalo" ("check it out"), the intonation sounds a bit off and unnatural. In a professional video, that's the kind of detail that would need fixing.

The good news is that ElevenLabs gives you very granular editing controls. You can tweak timing, pronunciation, tone, and phrasing. The interface is more advanced than most tools here and comes with a learning curve, but the control it gives you is worth it if audio quality is your priority.

Pricing

Pricing accurate as of April 2026.

ElevenLabs has a credit based system. The free plan includes 10,000 credits which will get you roughly 3-5 minutes of watermarked voiceover dubbing. The Creator plan is $22/month and with 10,0,000 you'll get 30-50 minutes of dubbing ($0.44-$0.73/minute).

Who should use it?

ElevenLabs is the best option if you don't need lip-sync and want the highest possible voiceover quality. It's particularly well suited to creators and professionals who are happy to spend time in the editor fine-tuning the output.

5. Maestra

Pros

  • Accurate translation with natural-sounding Spanish
  • Voice cloning that sounds just like me
  • Strong language coverage (125+ languages)

Cons

  • Confusing, frustrating interface
  • No voice cloning on the free plan
  • Lip-sync only available on the $159/month Business plan, charged at an additional $2/minute
  • Pricing structure only makes sense for large translation projects

My experience

Here's the translated video using Maestra:

Maestra_with_voice_cloning - Video generated with Synthesia

The translation accuracy was one of the best I tested. The Spanish sounded human and fluent, and in most cases it matched what I actually said.

That said, I didn't enjoy using it. The platform is not user-friendly, and it took me a long time to work out how to generate a translated video with a cloned voice. Simple tasks felt unnecessarily complicated.

Voice cloning isn't available on the free plan, which is a significant limitation. Once I paid and got it working, the cloned voice sounded pretty spot on. But I didn't get to test the lip-sync, as that requires the $199/month Business plan.

Pricing

Pricing accurate as of April 2026.

Maestra's voiceover plans start at $39/month (billed annually) for the Basic plan, which gives you 120 minutes of voiceover dubbing per month. Voice cloning becomes available on the Premium plan at $79/month, which includes 100 minutes of voice cloning per month. Lip-sync is available as an add-on on the Business plan at $159/month, charged at $2/minute on top of the plan cost.

Who should use it?

Maestra makes sense if you have a large volume of content to translate and want accurate, natural-sounding output at a reasonable per-minute rate. It's not a good fit if you only need to translate the occasional video, or if ease of use matters to you.

6. Kapwing

Pros

  • Affordable pricing
  • Good voice cloning
  • Lots of post-editing controls for subtitles, timing, and audio

Cons

  • Lip-sync quality isn't convincing
  • Videos must be at least 10 seconds long to run dubbing
  • Rough default export quality
  • Interface can feel overwhelming due to the volume of other editing features

My experience

Here's the translated video using Kapwing:

Kapwing_Lipsync - Video generated with Synthesia

Kapwing is primarily a video editor, and the dubbing and translation features sit alongside a lot of other functionality. That makes the interface feel overwhelming at first. It took me a moment to find the dubbing tools among everything else.

The translation itself was solid and subtitles were pretty accurate. The voice cloning sounded surprisingly good for a general-purpose video editor. The default export quality wasn't great though, and I noticed some roughness in the final video.

The lip-sync doesn't look that convincing. The timing and mouth movements aren't natural enough for professional use. One small but annoying limitation: videos must be at least 10 seconds long to run the dubbing feature, so I had to artificially extend my clip just to test it. Kapwing supports 40+ languages.

Pricing

Pricing accurate as of April 2026.

Kapwing offers a free plan with 30 seconds of dubbing. The Pro plan is $16/month (billed annually) and includes up to 50 minutes of dubbing at $0.32/minute. Lip-sync and voice cloning require the Business plan at $50/month (billed annually), which gives you up to 130 minutes at $0.38/minute.

Who should use it?

Kapwing makes sense if you need video translation and a broader set of video editing features in one tool. If lip-sync or dubbing quality is your priority, Synthesia, AI Studios, or ElevenLabs are better options.

7. Speechify

Pros

  • Supports dubbing into 60+ languages

Cons

  • No lip-sync
  • No voice cloning on the free plan
  • Translation accuracy issues that change the meaning of the original
  • Buggy platform that got stuck several times during testing
  • Overpriced for what you get

My experience

Here's the translated video using Speechify:

Speechify_with_voice_cloning - Video generated with Synthesia

Speechify appears to be a voiceover dubbing-only tool. I first tried the free plan, which doesn't include voice cloning. The first thing I noticed was that the translation wasn't quite accurate. My original line was "Check it out," which most tools translated as "Échale un vistazo." Speechify came out with "Echa un vistazo y verás cómo funciona," which adds "and you'll see how it works" to the end. That kind of unprompted addition can cause real problems in a high-stakes or client-facing translation.

I bought the Studio Starter plan to test voice cloning. Unlike every other tool on this list, Speechify requires you to clone your voice separately and then manually select it when translating. It's an unnecessary extra step. When I did get it working, the results sounded strange. Pronunciations were off in ways that would be noticeable to any native Spanish speaker.

The platform was also buggy and got stuck a few times during testing, which didn't help.

Pricing

Pricing accurate as of April 2026.

Speechify Studio offers a free plan with 600 credits, equivalent to around 3 minutes of dubbing. The Studio Starter plan is $19/month and gives you 40 minutes of dubbing at $0.48/minute, including access to voice cloning. The Studio Creator plan is $49/month and gives you 160 minutes of dubbing at $0.31/minute.

Who should use it?

Honestly, I'd skip Speechify and another tool instead. The translation accuracy issues and clunky voice cloning workflow make it hard to recommend over the other voiceover-only options on this list.

8. Murf

Pros

  • 100 minutes of free voiceover dubbing, the most generous free allowance on this list for voiceover
  • Supports 40+ languages

Cons

  • No video export on the free plan
  • Voiceover dubbing only, no lip-sync
  • Very limited language variations within each language
  • Voice cloning only available on the Enterprise plan

My experience

Here's the translated video using Murf:

MurfDub - Video generated with Synthesia

I was disappointed with Murf's dubbing solution. It's voiceover only, which is fair enough given that Murf is primarily an AI voice platform. But several other limitations make it hard to recommend.

The available language variations are very limited. For Spanish, you only get European and Mexican variants. That's a narrow set compared to most tools here. And while the free plan offers a generous 100 minutes of dubbing, you can't export your video without paying. I find that a frustrating way to structure a free tier.

The biggest issue is voice cloning. It's only available on the Enterprise plan, which means individual users and small teams are stuck with stock AI voices. The result doesn't sound like me at all, as you can hear in the video above.

Pricing

Pricing accurate as of April 2026.

Murf offers a free plan with 100 credits, equivalent to 50 minutes of dubbing, though exports include a watermark. To remove watermarks, you'll need the pay-as-you-go plan at $0.25/credit, which works out to $0.50/minute of dubbing.

Who should use it?

If you need free voiceover dubbing minutes and don't care about voice cloning or export, Murf's free plan is generous. For anything beyond that, there are better options on this list.

9. Veed

Pros

  • Accurate translation
  • Clean, easy-to-use interface with a broad set of video editing tools
  • Supports 125+ languages

Cons

  • No free dubbing or subtitles option
  • Lip-sync feels off
  • Voice cloning didn't work well in my test
  • Expensive relative to the output quality

My experience

Here's the translated video using Veed:

VEED_dubbing_lip_sync - Video generated with Synthesia

Veed doesn't offer any AI video translation on the free plan, so I had to pay just to test it. That's an immediate mark against it compared to most tools on this list.

The interface is clean and easy to use. There's a potential advantage in being able to translate and edit your video in one place, with access to animations, transitions, and other editing tools. But for most people translating videos, that extra functionality isn't worth the premium.

The translation accuracy was good. I particularly liked how the dubbed audio pronounced "Synthesia" the way a natural Spanish speaker would. But the voice cloning didn't really sound like me, and the lip-sync felt a bit off. Given that you're paying $49/month just to access these features, that's not good enough.

Pricing

Pricing accurate as of April 2026.

Veed's AI dubbing is available from the Pro plan at $49/month, which includes 30 minutes of dubbing per month at $1.63/minute. The Studio plan at $79/month includes 1 hour of dubbing per month at $1.32/minute. There is no free dubbing option.

Who should use it?

I don't recommend Veed for AI video translation. The output quality doesn't justify the price, especially when better free options exist. It might make sense if you already use Veed for video editing and want translation built into the same workflow.

10. Rask AI

Pros

  • Decent voice cloning quality
  • Wide language support (135+)

Cons

  • No video export on the free trial
  • Very high price for voiceover dubbing ($2.40/minute)
  • Lip-sync quality is genuinely poor
  • Lip-sync requires the most expensive plan ($150/month)

My experience

Here's the translated video using Rask AI:

Rask_AI - Video generated with Synthesia

Rask AI ended up being my least favourite tool on this list. The voice cloning itself wasn't bad, and the generated voice sounded fairly natural in isolation. On paper it also looks strong, with support for 135 languages.

In practice, the experience was frustrating. You can't download your translated video on the free plan. The pricing for voiceover dubbing is very high at $2.40/minute, which is roughly what you'd pay for full lip-synced dubbing on better platforms.

Rask's lip-sync is where things really fall apart. To access it you need the Creator Pro plan at $150/month. From the previews I tested, the quality was genuinely awful. The mouth movements were obviously mismatched and unnatural. It was bad enough that I didn't bother paying to export the result.

Pricing

Pricing accurate as of April 2026.

Rask AI's free trial includes 3 minutes of dubbing. The Creator plan starts at $60/month for 25 minutes of dubbing at $2.40/minute, or $50/month billed annually. The Creator Pro plan starts at $150/month for 100 minutes and adds lip-sync. Note that lip-sync counts as an additional minute per minute of video, so a 1-minute lip-synced video costs 2 minutes of your allowance.

Who should use it?

I don't recommend Rask AI. The lip-sync quality is too poor to be useful, and the voiceover dubbing is significantly overpriced compared to the other options on this list.

Which AI video translation tool should you use?

If you need the best possible quality and you're translating videos in a business context, Synthesia is the clear choice. The lip-sync is the most convincing I tested, the voice cloning is excellent, and the platform is built for teams that need to translate at scale.

If you're on a budget and lip-sync matters, start with AI Studios. The quality isn't quite professional grade, but the free plan is the most generous on this list and the per-minute rate is the lowest by some margin.

If you just need voiceover dubbing without lip-sync, ElevenLabs is the strongest option. The voice cloning is excellent and the editing controls give you a lot of precision over the final output.

For everyone else, Perso AI and Maestra are worth considering depending on your volume and budget. I'd avoid Rask AI, Veed, and Speechify altogether.

Frequently asked questions

What is the best AI video translation tool?

It depends on your needs. For business use, Synthesia is the strongest option: the lip-sync quality is the best I tested, and it has the features teams need to translate at scale. If you're on a budget and lip-sync still matters, AI Studios is the best value (it has up to 3 minutes free and the lowest per-minute rate on the list). For voiceover-only dubbing, ElevenLabs is very good.

How do I translate a video with AI using lip-sync and voice cloning?

Upload your video to a platform like Synthesia, select your target language (or languages), and the AI clones your voice and adjusts mouth movements to match your translated speech. Some tools also let you edit the translated transcript before finalizing, which is useful for fixing technical terms, product names, or anything where accuracy really matters. What makes Synthesia different is the secure editing feature, which prevents edits to the transcript that change its actual meaning. This is an important safeguard to preserve message integrity.

Can I translate a YouTube video with AI?

Yes. Synthesia lets you translate a YouTube video by pasting the URL directly into the tool. It transcribes, translates, and dubs the video with voice cloning and lip-sync without you needing to download or re-upload anything.

Is there a free AI video translator I can try?

Yes. Synthesia offers free dubbing with lip-sync for videos up to 1 minute. AI Studios is the most generous free option, with up to 3 minutes of lip-synced dubbing per month. Both let you download the translated video on the free plan, unlike Perso AI and Rask AI, which require a paid plan to export.

How does Synthesia compare to other video translation software for enterprise needs?

Synthesia is the only tool on this list built specifically for enterprise use. It supports bulk dubbing via API, has SSO-protected video pages, workspace-level permissions, and transcript editing controls that help legal and compliance teams protect meaning and regulatory language. If you need to translate entire content libraries rather than individual videos, it's the only option here that's genuinely designed for that.
